State Space Is Not Physical Space: Conservation, Geometry, and Planar Orbital Dynamics

In Reflection 1, my analysis focused entirely on the radial motion, which allowed me to understand how the distance from the central mass changes in time, including turning points and the distinction between inward and outward motion. Physically, this matched the intuition that a particle may move away from the Earth or fall toward it, and the “±” branches captured these two behaviors. However, as I noted in the first reflection, the system was not truly one-dimensional. Analyzing only $r(t)$ tells me how far the particle is from the origin but not where it is in the plane. Like two different objects could be equidistant from the origin, but actually at completely different locations in space. Without tracking the angular variable $\theta(t)$, the description of the motion is incomplete. So, based on my professor's feedback from Reflection 1, I'm now incorporating the angular component of the motion as well.

Deriving the Planar Model

To describe the motion in the plane, I use polar coordinates. The position of the particle is determined by two functions:

\[ r(t), \qquad \theta(t). \]

Here, \(r(t)\) measures the distance from the central mass, while \(\theta(t)\) records the angular position. In polar coordinates, the acceleration has radial and angular components

\[ a_r = \ddot r - r\dot\theta^2, \qquad a_\theta = r\ddot\theta + 2\dot r\dot\theta. \]

Since gravity acts only in the radial direction, Newton's second law gives

\[ m(\ddot r - r\dot\theta^2) = -\frac{GMm}{r^2}, \qquad m(r\ddot\theta + 2\dot r\dot\theta)=0. \]

The angular equation simplifies to

\[ r\ddot\theta + 2\dot r\dot\theta = 0. \]

Multiplying by \(r\), this becomes

\[ r^2\ddot\theta + 2r\dot r\dot\theta = 0. \]

But the left-hand side is exactly

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(r^2\dot\theta\right)=0. \]

Therefore,

\[ r^2\dot\theta = \ell, \]

where \(\ell\) is the conserved angular momentum per unit mass. Solving for \(\dot\theta\), we get

\[ \dot\theta = \frac{\ell}{r^2}. \]

Substituting this into the radial equation gives

\[ \ddot r - r\left(\frac{\ell}{r^2}\right)^2 = -\frac{GM}{r^2}. \]

Simplifying,

\[ \ddot r = \frac{\ell^2}{r^3} - \frac{GM}{r^2}. \]

Finally, introducing the radial velocity \(v=\dot r\), the system becomes

\[ \dot r = v, \qquad \dot v = \frac{\ell^2}{r^3} - \frac{GM}{r^2}, \qquad \dot\theta = \frac{\ell}{r^2}. \]

State Space Is Not Physical Space

At first glance, the system appears to be three-dimensional because it consists of three first-order differential equations:

\[ \dot r = v, \qquad \dot v = \frac{\ell^2}{r^3}-\frac{GM}{r^2}, \qquad \dot\theta = \frac{\ell}{r^2}. \]

When I first derived these equations, it was tempting to conclude that the motion had somehow become three-dimensional. After all, the system now contains three state variables. However, this interpretation kinda is misleading.

Physically, the particle still moves entirely within a two-dimensional plane. Nothing about the orbit has become three-dimensional. The additional dimension appears because the original second-order radial equation has been rewritten as a first-order system through the introduction of the velocity variable \(v=\dot r\).

In other words, state-space dimension counts how many quantities are needed to completely describe the evolution of the system, whereas physical dimension describes the geometry of the motion itself. These are related ideas, but they are not the same thing.

There is another subtle observation hiding inside the equations. Although the system formally contains three state variables, the angular equation

\[ \dot\theta=\frac{\ell}{r^2} \]

does not influence the radial dynamics. The variables \(r\) and \(v\) determine the evolution of the system, while \(\theta\) simply records where the particle happens to be along its orbit.

For this reason, I think of the angular equation as being somewhat "kinematic" in nature. It keeps track of how the orbit sweeps around the origin but it does not affect the structure of the phase portrait itself.

As a result, the qualitative dynamics can be studied entirely in the \((r,v)\)-plane. The angular equation will return later when discussing the geometry of the orbit and Kepler's laws, but for the phase-plane analysis it behaves more like a "bookkeeping differential equation" than active dynamical variable.

This realization was surprisingly memorable for me. Before working through this example, I had never thought carefully about the distinction between physical dimension and state-space dimension. Once I saw it here, it became difficult to unsee. The equations were describing a two-dimensional orbit using a three-dimensional state space, and that subtle distinction completely changed the way I think about dynamical systems.

Kepler's Laws from the Angular Equation

Although the angular equation does not affect the radial phase portrait in the \((r,v)\)-plane, it is not useless. In fact, it contains important geometric information about the orbit.

From the derivation above, angular momentum conservation gives

\[ r^2\dot\theta = \ell. \]

During a small angular displacement \(d\theta\), the radius vector sweeps out a small sector of area

\[ dA = \frac{1}{2}r^2\,d\theta. \]

Dividing by \(dt\), we obtain the rate at which area is swept out:

\[ \frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{1}{2}r^2\dot\theta. \]

Using \(r^2\dot\theta=\ell\), this becomes

\[ \frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{\ell}{2}. \]

Since \(\ell\) is constant, the area sweep rate is constant. This is exactly Kepler's Second Law: the radius vector sweeps out equal areas in equal times.

Kepler's First Law also emerges from the same polar-coordinate formulation. Substituting \(\dot\theta=\ell/r^2\) into the radial equation and introducing the standard change of variables \(u(\theta)=1/r\) leads to

\[ u'' + u = \frac{GM}{\ell^2}. \]

The full geometric solution of this equation leads into celestial mechanics and the theory of conic sections, which is beyond the scope of this note. However, the key point is that the familiar geometric laws of orbital motion are already encoded and emerges naturally while studying the dynamics.

The Reduced Planar System

Up to this point, the discussion has focused primarily on deriving the equations of motion. The next natural question is no longer how the system is obtained, but rather what the system does. This is where the viewpoint of dynamical systems becomes useful. Instead of searching for explicit solutions, the goal is to understand the qualitative behavior of all possible solutions at once.

As discussed in the previous section, the angular equation does not influence the radial dynamics. Therefore the qualitative behavior is completely determined by the reduced system

\[ \dot r = v, \qquad \dot v = \frac{\ell^2}{r^3} - \frac{GM}{r^2}. \]

Nullclines and Equilibrium

A useful first step in understanding a nonlinear system is locating its nullclines. The \(r\)-nullcline is obtained from \(\dot r=0\), giving

\[ v=0. \]

The \(v\)-nullcline is obtained from \(\dot v=0\):

\[ \frac{\ell^2}{r^3} - \frac{GM}{r^2} = 0. \]

Assuming \(r>0\), this simplifies to

\[ r_0 = \frac{\ell^2}{GM}. \]

The unique equilibrium point occurs at the intersection of the two nullclines:

\[ (r_0,v_0) = \left( \frac{\ell^2}{GM}, 0 \right). \]

Linearization and Center Behavior

To understand the local behavior near the equilibrium, I linearize the system by computing the Jacobian matrix of the vector field.

Let

\[ f(r,v)=v, \qquad g(r,v) = \frac{\ell^2}{r^3} - \frac{GM}{r^2}. \]

The Jacobian is

\[ J(r,v) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} & \frac{\partial f}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial g}{\partial r} & \frac{\partial g}{\partial v} \end{pmatrix}. \]

Computing the partial derivatives gives

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial r}=0, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial v}=1, \]

\[ \frac{\partial g}{\partial r} = -\frac{3\ell^2}{r^4} + \frac{2GM}{r^3}, \qquad \frac{\partial g}{\partial v}=0. \]

Evaluating at the equilibrium \(r_0=\ell^2/(GM)\) yields

\[ J(r_0,v_0) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1\\ -\frac{(GM)^4}{\ell^6} & 0 \end{pmatrix}. \]

The characteristic equation becomes

\[ \lambda^2 + \frac{(GM)^4}{\ell^6} = 0, \]

with eigenvalues

\[ \lambda_{1,2} = \pm i\frac{(GM)^2}{\ell^3}. \]

The eigenvalues are purely imaginary. Equivalently,

\[ \operatorname{tr}(J)=0, \qquad \det(J)>0. \]

Both viewpoints lead to the same conclusion: the equilibrium is a center. Nearby trajectories neither converge toward the equilibrium nor diverge away from it. Instead, they move around it in closed loops.

Phase Portrait and Center Dynamics

This classification is easier to understand visually through the phase portrait. The vector field suggests rotation around the equilibrium, and the sample trajectories form closed curves rather than spiraling inward or outward.

Phase portrait in the \((r,v)\)-plane.

The phase portrait suggests that the equilibrium behaves like a center. Nearby trajectories form closed curves surrounding the fixed point, and the linearization predicts purely oscillatory motion. At first glance, it is tempting to conclude that the nonlinear system behaves exactly like its linear approximation.

Hartman–Grobman and Its Failure

This is precisely where one of the most important theorems in dynamical systems becomes relevant. The Hartman–Grobman Theorem states that near a hyperbolic equilibrium point, the nonlinear system is topologically equivalent to its linearization. In other words, the local behavior can be understood completely by studying the Jacobian matrix.

The key hypothesis is hyperbolicity. An equilibrium is hyperbolic only when none of the eigenvalues of the Jacobian have zero real part.

In the present system, the eigenvalues are

\[ \lambda = \pm i\sqrt{\frac{GM}{\hat r^3}}, \]

which are purely imaginary. Their real parts are exactly zero. Consequently, the equilibrium is non-hyperbolic, and the Hartman–Grobman Theorem does not apply.

This means that the phase portrait shown above cannot be justified solely from the linearization. The Jacobian correctly suggests rotational behavior, but it does not explain why trajectories remain on closed curves rather than spiraling inward or outward. To understand that structure, an additional idea is needed.

That idea comes from conservation. The system possesses a conserved energy, and as will be seen in the next section, the geometry of the phase portrait is ultimately determined by the level sets of that energy function.

Conservation Creates Geometry

Since the Hartman-Grobman Theorem does not apply, linearization alone cannot explain the closed curves in the phase portrait. To understand why the trajectories remain trapped on loops, we need another source of structure. A natural question is whether the system has a conserved quantity.

Starting from the reduced system,

\[ \dot r = v, \qquad \dot v = \frac{\ell^2}{r^3} - \frac{GM}{r^2}, \]

we can eliminate time by writing

\[ \frac{dv}{dr} = \frac{\dot v}{\dot r} = \frac{ \frac{\ell^2}{r^3} - \frac{GM}{r^2} }{v}. \]

Multiplying through gives

\[ v\,dv - \left( \frac{\ell^2}{r^3} - \frac{GM}{r^2} \right)dr = 0. \]

This has the form

\[ P(r,v)\,dr + Q(r,v)\,dv = 0, \]

where

\[ P(r,v) = -\frac{\ell^2}{r^3} + \frac{GM}{r^2}, \qquad Q(r,v)=v. \]

The exactness condition is

\[ \frac{\partial P}{\partial v} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial r}. \]

Here,

\[ \frac{\partial P}{\partial v}=0, \qquad \frac{\partial Q}{\partial r}=0. \]

Therefore the differential form is exact, so there exists a function \(E(r,v)\) satisfying

\[ dE = P(r,v)\,dr + Q(r,v)\,dv. \]

To find \(E\), integrate \(Q(r,v)=v\) with respect to \(v\):

\[ E(r,v) = \frac{1}{2}v^2 + f(r). \]

Differentiating with respect to \(r\) gives

\[ \frac{\partial E}{\partial r} = f'(r). \]

Matching this with \(P(r,v)\), we get

\[ f'(r) = -\frac{\ell^2}{r^3} + \frac{GM}{r^2}. \]

Integrating,

\[ f(r) = \frac{\ell^2}{2r^2} - \frac{GM}{r}. \]

Thus the conserved quantity is

\[ E(r,v) = \frac{1}{2}v^2 + \frac{\ell^2}{2r^2} - \frac{GM}{r}. \]

The significance of this calculation is not just that a conserved quantity exists. It explains the geometry of the phase portrait. Since \(E(r,v)\) remains constant along trajectories, solutions are confined to level curves of \(E\). The closed orbits seen in the phase portrait are therefore not accidental but they are a direct consequence of the conserved structure of the system.

This is where the earlier phase portrait becomes much less mysterious. If \(E(r,v)\) is constant along trajectories, then solutions cannot move freely through the \((r,v)\)-plane. They must stay on the level curves of \(E\).

Energy contours of the reduced orbital system

This explains why the equilibrium behaves like a center. The linearization suggested circular motion through purely imaginary eigenvalues, but Hartman-Grobman could not justify the nonlinear phase portrait because the equilibrium is non-hyperbolic. The conserved quantity fills in the missing structure, which is solutions remain trapped on closed energy contours around the equilibrium instead of spiraling inward or outward.

Limit Cycles and the Poincaré–Bendixson Theorem

The energy contour picture also explains why the closed curves in the phase portrait are not limit cycles. A limit cycle is an isolated periodic orbit that attracts or repels nearby trajectories. In the present system, this cannot happen because every solution remains on its own energy level set. Since the energy is conserved, trajectories cannot slowly spiral toward a preferred orbit or drift away from it. Instead, each initial condition determines a different closed curve, and the motion remains confined to that curve for all time.

The distinction is subtle but important. The closed trajectories surrounding the equilibrium are periodic orbits, but they are not limit cycles. Nothing attracts nearby trajectories, and nothing repels them. The collection of closed curves arises entirely from conservation of energy.

The planar nature of the system imposes an additional restriction. The Poincaré–Bendixson Theorem states that sufficiently well-behaved bounded trajectories in two-dimensional systems can only approach a small number of possible long-term behaviors, namely equilibria or periodic orbits.

In the present setting, the theorem does not reveal anything surprising because the conservation law already confines trajectories to closed energy contours, and the phase portrait shows exactly what the theorem predicts: solutions repeatedly travel around closed loops without approaching the equilibrium and without exhibiting more complicated behavior.

What I found interesting is that the theorem also highlights a limitation of planar systems. Once energy conservation and the two-dimensional geometry are taken into account, there is no chance for genuinely complicated long-term dynamics like chaos. The motion is structured, predictable, and periodic.

Lyapunov Stability of the Equilibrium

The energy function provides a natural Lyapunov function for the equilibrium. Since the equilibrium does not occur at zero energy, it is convenient to shift the energy by a constant and define

\[ V(r,v)=E(r,v)-E(\hat r,0). \]

This shift changes neither the physics nor the geometry of the system. It simply normalizes the equilibrium so that

\[ V(\hat r,0)=0. \]

Furthermore, \(V(r,v)\) is positive for nearby points and remains constant along trajectories. Consequently, the equilibrium is Lyapunov stable, which means solutions that begin sufficiently close to the equilibrium remain close for all future time.

However, the equilibrium is not asymptotically stable. Since the energy never decreases, trajectories cannot approach the equilibrium as time increases. Instead, they continue moving around the surrounding energy contours indefinitely. And this is exactly the behavior expected from a linearized-center phase portrait.

At this point, the qualitative picture is essentially complete. Energy conservation explains the closed curves, Lyapunov theory explains the stability of the equilibrium, and the Poincaré–Bendixson Theorem confirms that no more complicated long-term behavior can occur within this planar setting.

Naturally, this raises a new question:

If two-dimensional systems are so heavily constrained, where does chaos come from?

That question became the starting point for my next reflection, where I began thinking about higher-dimensional systems, sensitive dependence on initial conditions, and the first ideas that eventually led me toward chaos.
