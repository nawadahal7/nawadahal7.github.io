Research

My research lives at the intersection of numerical analysis, nonlinear partial differential equations, and scientific computing. Across different projects, I am interested in understanding how numerical methods behave: why they converge, when they fail, how finite precision affects computation, and how mathematical analysis can guide better algorithms for physically motivated problems.

Current Research Program

Semiconductor Modeling, Volume I: The Poisson-Boltzmann Equation

Honors thesis completed / Poster presented / Ongoing extensions

This project studies finite-difference discretizations and iterative methods for nonlinear Poisson-Boltzmann equations arising in semiconductor modeling. The work includes the analysis of fixed-point, Newton, and quasi-linearization methods, together with MATLAB implementations and numerical experiments.

A research poster based on this project was presented at San Jose State University Research Day: Research Poster (PDF).

An honors thesis based on this work has been completed. Portions of the project are being developed further as part of ongoing research, so the thesis is not currently posted online.

Semiconductor Modeling, Volume II: Solar-Cell Simulation

Ongoing

This project extends my previous semiconductor drift-diffusion-Poisson modeling work to photovoltaic and solar-cell applications. Current interests include photogeneration, carrier transport, recombination mechanisms, current-voltage characteristics, power generation, fill factor, and device efficiency.

Completed and Submitted Work

A Matrix-Based Perspective on Multi-Level Richardson Extrapolation

Manuscript submitted to SIAM Undergraduate Research Online (SIURO)

This project investigates multi-level Richardson extrapolation methods for numerical integration. The work focuses on error reduction, finite-precision effects, conditioning, and numerical stability.

What started as a numerical integration project became a study of how extrapolation behaves under finite precision. In exact arithmetic, adding more extrapolation levels can improve the order of accuracy. In floating-point arithmetic, however, the weights can grow, conditioning can deteriorate, and roundoff error can eventually dominate.

Future Directions

Some directions I am currently interested in exploring further include asymptotic analysis, singular perturbation methods, numerical methods for nonlinear PDEs, dynamical systems, and mathematical models arising from physics.
