A Semester Thinking About Dynamical Systems

During Fall 2025, I took my first course in dynamical systems. The course began with one-dimensional flows, moved into planar systems, and ended with a brief introduction to chaos. What stayed with me was not only the individual tools, but the way the subject forced me to think about dimension, phase space, conserved quantities, stability, and perturbation growth.

1. When a One-Dimensional System Is Not Quite One-Dimensional

In my first reflection, I studied radial motion in a gravitational field using an energy-based reduction. The equation involved a single state variable $r$, but solving for $\dot r$ introduced two branches: outward motion and inward motion.

2. State Space Is Not the Same as Physical Space

In the second reflection, I extended the model by adding angular motion. The particle still moves in a physical plane, but after rewriting the equations as a first-order system, the state variables are not simply the physical coordinates.

[Write about the distinction between physical dimension and state-space dimension. This is one of the strongest ideas from your Reflection II.]

A possible reduced planar system:

\[ \dot r = v, \qquad \dot v = \frac{\ell^2}{r^3} - \frac{GM}{r^2}. \]

3. Conservation, Centers, and Why Nothing Spirals In

One thing that fascinated me was how conserved quantities change the qualitative behavior of a system. In a dissipative system, trajectories may spiral into an attractor. In a conservative system, trajectories can remain trapped on level sets of an energy-like quantity.

[Write about the conserved energy function from Reflection II, why the center is stable but not asymptotically stable, and why these closed orbits are not limit cycles.]

A possible energy-like quantity:

\[ E(r,v) = \frac{1}{2}v^2 + \frac{\ell^2}{2r^2} - \frac{GM}{r}. \]
