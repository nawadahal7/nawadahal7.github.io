A Semester Thinking About Dynamical Systems

During Fall 2025, I took my first course in Dynamical Systems. Up to that point, most of my experience with differential equations came from lower-division courses, where the emphasis was largely on finding explicit solutions. Like, given an equation, the goal was usually to solve it.

The course emphasized qualitative behavior: fixed points, stability, phase portraits, bifurcations, and the overall structure of the solution space. One of the biggest conceptual shifts for me was realizing that solving a differential equation is not always the most interesting question. During the first few weeks, I often found myself wanting to separate variables, find a closed-form solution, or compute an exact answer. Letting go of that instinct was difficult.

Over time, I began to appreciate a different perspective. Rather than asking “Can I solve this equation?”, I started asking questions such as “What do all solutions do?”, “Which qualitative behaviors are possible?”, and “How does the geometry of the system determine its dynamics?” Looking back, that transition from computation to qualitative reasoning was probably the most influential part of the course for me. It changed the way I think about differential equations and played an important role in my growing interest in dynamical systems, PDEs, and applied mathematics more broadly.

The notes below grew out of three course reflections written throughout the semester. In those reflections, students were asked to discuss strengths, weaknesses, plans for improvement, and an application of their choice related to the material. The discussions presented here are not the original submissions. Instead, they are revised and expanded versions that preserve the main ideas, applications, and questions that stayed with me even after the course ended.

When a One-Dimensional System Isn't Quite One-Dimensional: A Dynamical Systems View of Radial Gravitational Motion

One of the first applications I explored in Dynamical Systems involved the radial motion of a particle moving under gravity. At first glance, this appears to be a completely ordinary one-dimensional problem. The particle moves closer to or farther away from a central mass (Earth), and the only state variable is the distance from the origin, usually denoted by $r$.

My goal was not to solve the problem exactly, but to understand it from a dynamical systems perspective. Rather than focusing on an explicit solution, I wanted to understand the qualitative behavior of the motion: where trajectories could go, whether turning points existed, and how the structure of the system depended on the total mechanical energy.

Energy Changes Everything

Starting from Newton's law of gravitation, the equation of motion can be written as

\[ m\ddot r = -\frac{GMm}{r^2}. \]

Multiplying both sides by $\dot r$ and integrating leads directly to conservation of mechanical energy,

\[ E = \frac12 m\dot r^2 - \frac{GMm}{r}. \]

Interpreting the terms, the first term represents kinetic energy, while the second represents gravitational potential energy and hence, their sum is the total mechanical energy of the system. Since $E$ appears only as an integration constant, the equation immediately reveals one of the most fundamental principles in classical mechanics: the conservation of mechanical energy.

This was the first moment where the problem became genuinely interesting to me. Instead of working directly with a second-order differential equation, the motion could now be understood through the geometry and analysis of the energy function $E$. Rather than asking for an explicit solution, I could begin asking qualitative questions like Which regions of motion are physically accessible? Where do turning points occur? How does the behavior change as the total energy varies? In a sense, the differential equation had been transformed into a geometric object whose structure already contained much of the information about the motion.

Physical Domain and Admissible Motion

Before discussing existence, uniqueness, or stability, it is worth thinking about the domain of the problem itself. Since $r$ represents the distance from a particle to the central mass, only positive values of $r$ are physically meaningful. Negative values of $r$ would correspond to a negative distance, which has no interpretation in this physical setting.

The energy formulation also reveals an additional restriction. From

\[ \dot r = \pm \sqrt{\frac{2}{m}\left(E+\frac{GMm}{r}\right)} \]

the quantity inside the square root must be nonnegative. This requirement determines which regions of space are accessible for a particle with a given total energy.

For positive energy, $E>0$, the particle has more than enough energy to escape the gravitational attraction of the central mass. In the case of a projectile launched from Earth, this corresponds to launching the object faster than the escape velocity. Although gravity continues to pull on the particle, it never has enough influence to bring it back. The particle continues moving farther away indefinitely.

The case $E=0$ is particularly interesting. Here, the particle possesses exactly the amount of energy required to escape. If launched from Earth, it would travel farther and farther away while continuously slowing down under the influence of gravity. However, it would never stop and return. In the limit as $r \to \infty$, both the kinetic and gravitational potential energies approach zero. The particle escapes, but only barely. For this reason, $E=0$ represents the threshold between returning motion and escape motion.

For negative energy, $E<0$, the situation is different. The particle does not have enough energy to escape. As it moves away from the central mass, its kinetic energy decreases until it eventually reaches zero at some finite radius. This point is called a turning point. Beyond this radius, motion is no longer physically possible because the quantity inside the square root becomes negative. The particle therefore reverses direction and begins falling back toward the Earth, in other words motion remains gravitationally bounded.

What I found interesting here is that the energy does much more than provide a conserved quantity. It also determines the geometry of the physically accessible region. Before solving anything, the energy already tells us whether a particle will eventually return, escape forever, or exist exactly on the boundary between the two possibilities.

Existence, Uniquenes and A Interesting Failure of Uniquness

The admissible domain analysis from the previous section also provides a natural starting point for discussing existence and uniqueness of solutions. Since the quantity inside the square root remains nonnegative throughout the physically accessible region, the vector field is continuous wherever the motion is defined. By the Existence Theorem, a solution therefore exists through any admissible initial condition.

To investigate uniqueness, we consider the differential equation

\[ \dot r = f(r) = \pm \sqrt{\frac{2}{m} \left( E+\frac{GMm}{r} \right)}. \]

The derivative of the vector field is

\[ f'(r) = \pm \frac{GM} {r^2 \sqrt{\frac{2}{m} \left( E+\frac{GMm}{r} \right)}}. \]

Whenever

\[ E+\frac{GMm}{r} > 0, \]

the denominator remains finite and the derivative is bounded on any closed interval away from \(r=0\). Consequently, the vector field is locally Lipschitz and uniqueness follows from the standard Existence and Uniqueness Theorem. Physically, this means that once the initial position and direction of motion are specified, the future trajectory is completely determined.

Something interesting happens at the turning point. Recall from the previous section that for negative energy \(E<0\), there exists a radius

\[ r_{\text{turn}} = -\frac{GMm}{E}, \]

where the radial velocity vanishes. At this point,

\[ E+\frac{GMm}{r_{\text{turn}}}=0. \]

As a result, the denominator of \(f'(r)\) becomes zero and the derivative blows up. The vector field remains continuous, so existence of solutions is still guaranteed, but local Lipschitz continuity fails. Consequently, the standard uniqueness theorem no longer applies.

What I found fascinating is that this mathematical failure occurs precisely at the most physically meaningful point in the motion. The turning point corresponds to the instant when a projectile reaches its maximum height above the Earth. For a brief moment the radial velocity vanishes, after which the motion reverses direction and the particle begins falling back toward the central mass.

Mathematics and Physics are telling the same story from different perspectives. The point where the uniqueness theorem fails to apply is exactly the point where the trajectory changes direction (aka turning point of projectile motion). Therefore, the failure of Lipschitz continuity reflects a genuine physical transition in the motion itself, which is beautiful interpretation, by the way.

Qualitative Behavior and Fixed Points

The next natural question is: what do those solutions actually do? Rather than attempting to solve the differential equation explicitly, a dynamical systems approach begins by looking for fixed points and studying the qualitative behavior of trajectories near them.

Fixed points occur whenever

\[ \dot r = 0. \]

Using

\[ \dot r = \pm \sqrt{\frac{2}{m} \left( E+\frac{GMm}{r} \right)}, \]

it follows that fixed points satisfy

\[ E+\frac{GMm}{r}=0. \]

Solving for \(r\) gives

\[ \hat r = -\frac{GMm}{E}. \]

The existence of this fixed point depends entirely on the sign of the energy. When \(E>0\), the resulting value of \(\hat r\) is negative and therefore has no physical meaning. When \(E=0\), no finite fixed point exists. Only when \(E<0\) do we obtain a physically meaningful equilibrium radius,

\[ \hat r = \frac{GMm}{|E|}. \]

This observation is consistent with the discussion from the previous section. Negative energy corresponds to returning motion, and the fixed point appears precisely at the turning point where the particle reaches its maximum distance from the central mass before reversing direction.

Why Linearization Fails

In most one-dimensional dynamical systems, stability is determined by evaluating the derivative of the vector field at the fixed point. If the derivative is negative, the equilibrium is stable. If the derivative is positive, the equilibrium is unstable.

For the present system, however, this approach immediately runs into a problem. The derivative of the vector field is

\[ f'(r) = \pm \frac{GM} {r^2 \sqrt{\frac{2}{m} \left( E+\frac{GMm}{r} \right)}}. \]

At the fixed point \(\hat r\), the quantity inside the square root vanishes. Consequently, the denominator becomes zero and the derivative blows up. The standard linearization test therefore fails completely.

Rather than using linearization, the stability must be determined directly from the sign of the vector field itself.

Phase-Line Analysis

Since linearization is unavailable, the behavior of solutions can be understood through a phase-line analysis. For \(0 < r < \hat r\), the quantity inside the square root remains positive, so the sign of \(\dot r\) is determined entirely by the choice of branch.

On the positive branch,

\[ \dot r > 0, \]

and trajectories move outward toward the turning point. On the negative branch,

\[ \dot r < 0, \]

and trajectories move inward toward the central mass.

The turning point therefore acts as a boundary of the motion. Outward trajectories eventually reach \(\hat r\), where the velocity vanishes and the motion reverses direction. The particle then transitions to the inward branch and begins falling back toward the Earth.

Why This Isn't Quite One-Dimensional

At first glance, this appears to be an ordinary one-dimensional autonomous differential equation. After all, the only state variable is \(r\), the distance from the particle to the Earth.

However, something subtle is happening. The energy formulation produces two possible vector fields,

\[ \dot r = + \sqrt{\frac{2}{m} \left( E+\frac{GMm}{r} \right)} \]

and

\[ \dot r = - \sqrt{\frac{2}{m} \left( E+\frac{GMm}{r} \right)}. \]

For the same admissible value of \(r\), there are therefore two possible directions of motion: outward motion and inward motion. The positive branch describes a particle moving away from the central mass, while the negative branch describes a particle moving toward it.

This observation led to one of the most interesting discussions I had while working on this problem. Although the model only contains a single state variable, it does not behave like the standard one-dimensional flows studied in class. Instead, the positive and negative branches act like two separate one-dimensional systems running side by side.

The model is therefore in a strange middle ground. It is not genuinely two-dimensional, since there is still only one state variable. Yet it is also not quite the usual one-dimensional autonomous flow. The turning point acts as the place where the motion switches from one branch to the other, connecting the outward and inward dynamics into a single physical trajectory.

Looking back, this was the part of the project that stayed with me the most. What began as a simple gravitational model eventually became a question about the structure of dynamical systems themselves and what it really means for a system to be "one-dimensional."
